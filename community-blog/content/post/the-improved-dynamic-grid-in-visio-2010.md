**First published on MSDN on Dec, 08 2009**

In Visio 2010, we've made it easier to align and space shapes to make
your diagrams look neat and organized. In a previous post we covered our
new [layout
improvements](http://blogs.msdn.com/visio/archive/2009/08/18/layout-improvements-in-visio-2010.aspx)
that help clean up existing diagrams. In this post, we're going to cover
how improvements in Visio's Dynamic Grid can help you drag a single
shape and more easily position it in relation to other shapes on the
page.

### **Turning On the Dynamic Grid** {#toc-hId-1451051647}

The Dynamic Grid is turned on by default for most diagram templates. You
can turn it on or off by toggling the checkbox in the **View** tab:

[[![](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/t5/image/serverpage/image-id/44015i4C3A751BD1E9E8F5/image-size/large?v=v2&px=999){.lia-media-image

### **Aligning shapes** {#toc-hId--1101105314}

To see the Dynamic Grid in action, simply drop a shape next to another
shape and notice the orange lines that automatically appear:

[[![](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/t5/image/serverpage/image-id/44016iBBB6B4CB9DC5863E/image-size/large?v=v2&px=999){.lia-media-image

In the example above, Visio detected that the selected shape matched the
centerline of a nearby shape. If the shapes were of different sizes,
Visio would attempt to align the shapes based on edge boundaries such as
the top, bottom, left, or right edges of shapes in addition to the
centerline:

[[![](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/t5/image/serverpage/image-id/44017iC5212EFC8AB6CC5B/image-size/large?v=v2&px=999){.lia-media-image

Note that the Dynamic Grid only aligns the same edge boundaries of
shapes. For example, a top edge of a shape will snap to the top edge of
another shape, but not to the bottom edge of another shape.

### **Spacing shapes** {#toc-hId-641705021}

The Dynamic Grid also displays orange line segments when evenly spaced
shapes are found close to each other. This is useful for easily placing
shapes in equal distances from one another. Simply drop a shape next to
other evenly spaced shapes to see these line segments in action:

[[![](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/t5/image/serverpage/image-id/44018iB2C752405A9211C5/image-size/large?v=v2&px=999){.lia-media-image

The Dynamic Grid scans both horizontally and vertically when displaying
feedback. This allows you to easily position shapes in relation to many
surrounding shapes:

[[![](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/t5/image/serverpage/image-id/44019iF25FCA8E7D345170/image-size/large?v=v2&px=999){.lia-media-image

### **Snapping to the Dynamic Grid** {#toc-hId--1910451940}

When either an alignment or spacing relationship is found between
shapes, Visio will gently snap the shape you have selected to an
invisible grid. This snapping behavior makes it easy to grab a shape
using the mouse and position it next to other shapes.

### **Page margins and centerlines** {#toc-hId--167641605}

To help position shapes within a page, the Dynamic Grid also supports
margins around pages. You can snap to page margins by simply dragging a
shape towards the top, bottom, left or right margins of a page. If the
page is completely empty, you can also snap to the center of the page.

### Container margins and centerlines {#toc-hId-1575168730}

To help position shapes within a
[container](http://blogs.msdn.com/visio/archive/2009/08/25/organizing-diagrams-with-containers.aspx)
, the Dynamic Grid also supports container margins and centerlines. You
can snap to containers, such as
[swimlanes](http://blogs.msdn.com/visio/archive/2009/09/01/cross-functional-flowcharts-in-visio-2010.aspx)
, by simply dragging a shape around the container margin or in the
center of the container:
